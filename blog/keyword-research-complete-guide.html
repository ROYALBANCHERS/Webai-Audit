So, What Exactly is Keyword Research?

The Simple Definition

Keyword research is the process of finding and analyzing the actual terms people type into search engines. Sounds straightforward, right? But here's why it matters: it helps you understand what your target audience is looking for, how often they're searching for it, and how difficult it'll be to rank for those terms. Basically, it's market research for the digital age.

Discover

Find out what your audience is actually searching for

Analyze

Evaluate how tough the competition is and how much traffic you could get

Target

Choose the best keywords to go after based on opportunity

Not All Keywords Are Created Equal

1 Short-Tail Keywords (aka Head Terms)

These are your 1-2 word broad terms. Tons of search volume, but crazy competitive. Unless you're a big brand, good luck ranking for these.

"shoes" "coffee" "insurance"

2 Mid-Tail Keywords

A bit more specific, usually 2-3 words. Still competitive, but more manageable than head terms.

"running shoes for men" "organic coffee beans" "car insurance quotes"

3 Long-Tail Keywords (The Sweet Spot)

4+ words, lower search volume, way less competition—and much higher conversion rates. These are your best friends, especially when you're starting out.

"best running shoes for flat feet men" "organic fair trade coffee beans online"

4 LSI Keywords (Related Terms)

These aren't synonyms exactly—they're related terms that help search engines understand context. Using them makes your content more comprehensive.

"sneakers" (for shoes) "espresso" (for coffee)

Understanding Search Intent

Here's something a lot of people get wrong: Google cares more about WHY someone is searching than WHAT they're searching for. This is called search intent, and matching it is crucial for ranking.

Informational Intent

The searcher wants to learn something. They're in research mode.

"how to tie a tie" "best SEO practices" "what is javascript"

Navigational Intent

They know exactly where they want to go. They're using Google as a shortcut.

"facebook login" "youtube" "gmail sign in"

Transactional Intent

They're ready to buy something. Cha-ching!

"buy nike shoes online" "best price laptop" "cheap flights to nyc"

Commercial Investigation

They're comparing options before making a decision. Still in consideration mode.

"nike vs adidas" "best laptop 2024" "seo tool reviews"

Making Sense of Keyword Metrics

Keyword tools throw a lot of numbers at you. Here's what actually matters and how to interpret it all.

Search Volume

This tells you how many times a keyword gets searched per month on average. But don't get too hung up on big numbers—sometimes lower volume means less competition and more qualified traffic.

High Volume

10,000+

Usually super competitive

Medium Volume

1,000-10,000

Moderate competition

Low Volume

100-1,000

Easier to rank for

Keyword Difficulty (KD)

Most tools give you a score from 0-100 estimating how tough it is to rank. Higher score = harder to rank. Keep in mind this is just an estimate—sometimes you can beat the competition with better content.

0-20

Should be doable

21-40

Moderate challenge

41-60

Getting competitive

61-100

Extremely difficult

CPC (Cost Per Click)

This shows what advertisers are paying per click. Why does it matter for SEO? High CPC usually means high commercial intent—people searching these terms are closer to buying.

$5+

High commercial value

$1-5

Moderate commercial value

Under $1

Lower commercial value

My Step-by-Step Keyword Research Process

How I Do Keyword Research (The Actual Process)

1

Brainstorm Seed Keywords

List out broad topics related to your business. Think about what your ideal customer would search for at different stages of their journey.

2

Plug Into Research Tools

Enter those seed keywords into your favorite tool to generate hundreds of related ideas along with all those important metrics.

3

Check Out the Competition

See what keywords your competitors are ranking for. Sometimes you'll find opportunities they're overlooking.

4

Match Search Intent

For each keyword, ask yourself: what is the searcher actually looking for? Does your content answer that?

5

Prioritize Based on Opportunity

Look for the sweet spot: decent search volume + manageable difficulty + high relevance to your business.

6

Group and Map to Content

Organize related keywords into clusters and map them to specific pieces of content you'll create.

Keyword Research Tools Worth Knowing

Google Keyword Planner

Free tool built into Google Ads. Great for getting started, though data ranges can be annoying.

Best for: Beginners and those on a budget

Pros: Free, straight from Google

Cons: Limited features, shows ranges instead of exact numbers

Ahrefs

Comprehensive SEO suite with seriously good keyword research capabilities.

Best for: Serious SEOs who need accurate data

Pros: Super accurate data, great competitor insights

Cons: Not cheap (but worth it)

SEMrush

All-in-one marketing toolkit with a killer Keyword Magic Tool.

Best for: Marketing teams and agencies

Pros: Feature-rich, solid data accuracy

Cons: Premium pricing (you get what you pay for)

Answer The Public

Visualizes all the questions people are asking around your keywords. Great for content ideas.

Best for: Content ideation and finding question keywords

Pros: Visual approach, finds questions you wouldn't think of

Cons: Limited free searches per day

Screaming Frog

Website crawler that helps uncover keyword opportunities on your own site.

Best for: Technical SEO and site audits

Pros: Deep site analysis capabilities

Cons: Definitely has a learning curve

Ubersuggest

User-friendly keyword research tool that also suggests content ideas.

Best for: Small business owners and beginners

Pros: Easy to use, relatively affordable

Cons: Data isn't as accurate as the premium tools

Competitor Keyword Analysis

Want to know a secret? Your competitors have already done a lot of the hard work. Let's learn from them.

Finding Your Competitors' Keywords

Identify Who You're Up Against

Search your target keywords and see who's showing up on page one—those are your SEO competitors.

Use SEO Tools to Spy

Plug competitor domains into Ahrefs or SEMrush to see exactly what keywords they're ranking for.

Find Content Gaps

Look for keywords competitors rank for that you don't—these are golden opportunities.

Analyze Their Content

What's working for them? Can you create something even better? That's the Skyscraper Technique in action.

Why Long-Tail Keywords Are Your Friend

The Long-Tail Advantage

  • Way less competition (easier to rank)
  • Higher conversion rates (people know what they want)
  • More specific search intent
  • Better for voice search optimization
  • Easier to create targeted content around
  • Less expensive for PPC if you go that route

Where to Find Long-Tail Keywords

Google Autocomplete

Start typing your seed term and see what Google suggests—these are real searches people make.

"People Also Ask"

Those question boxes in SERPs are gold mines for long-tail ideas.

Related Searches

Scroll to the bottom of Google's results page for more ideas.

Question-Based Tools

Answer The Public and AlsoAsked are built specifically for this.

Keyword Grouping & Clustering

The Topic Cluster Strategy

Instead of creating random pages, organize your keywords into topic clusters. It's better for users and Google loves it:

Link Building

Local SEO

Each cluster page links back to the pillar, creating a topical authority signal that tells Google you really know this stuff.

Tracking Your Progress

You can't improve what you don't measure. Here's how to keep track of how your keywords are performing.

What to Track

  • Keyword positions over time (are you moving up?)
  • Organic traffic changes from each keyword
  • Click-through rates (are people clicking?)
  • Featured snippet wins (those position zero spots)
  • Competitor movements (are they gaining on you?)
  • SERP features your pages show up in
