On-Page SEO Complete Guide

Optimize every page for maximum search visibility

60 min read Updated Jan 20, 2025

Let's talk about on-page SEO—the stuff you actually control on your own website. You know, unlike those mysterious backlinks everyone's always chasing? On-page SEO (sometimes called on-site SEO) is all about optimizing each individual page so Google actually understands what it's about and decides it's worth showing to people. While off-page SEO is all about external signals you can't really control, on-page SEO is where you get to fine-tune both your content and the actual HTML code. Pretty empowering when you think about it. In this guide, I'm going to walk you through everything you need to know to nail your on-page optimization game.

What We'll Cover

Title Tag Optimization Meta Descriptions Heading Structure (H1-H6) Content Optimization Strategic Keyword Placement Image Optimization URL Optimization Internal Linking External Linking Page Speed & Core Web Vitals Mobile Optimization User Experience Signals

Title Tags: Your First Impression

Here's the thing about title tags—they're hands down the most important on-page SEO element you've got. Think about it: this is what shows up in search results, browser tabs, and when people share your stuff on social media. No pressure, right? But seriously, getting this right can make a huge difference in whether people click through to your site or keep scrolling.

Title Tag Best Practices

Keep It Concise

50-60 characters is the sweet spot (about 600 pixels)

Front-Load Keywords

Put your main keyword near the beginning

Add Your Brand

Stick your brand name at the end

Make It Unique

Every page deserves its own distinct title

Nailed It

Best Running Shoes for Flat Feet | Nike Official

Clear, descriptive, keyword-rich, and branded

Don't Do This

Running Shoes Walking Shoes Tennis Shoes Sneakers Athletics Sports Fitness Jogging

Classic keyword stuffing, way too long

Pro Title Tag Formula

Follow this structure and you can't go wrong:

[Primary Keyword] + [Secondary Keyword] | [Brand Name]

Meta Descriptions: Your Elevator Pitch

Alright, here's something that might surprise you: meta descriptions aren't actually a direct ranking factor anymore. But don't go ignoring them! They still matter—a lot. Why? Because they're your pitch to searchers. A great meta description can dramatically boost your click-through rates, which indirectly helps your rankings. Think of it as your two-second chance to convince someone your page is worth their time.

Meta Description Checklist

Keep it around 150-160 characters
Include your keyword naturally
Write something actually compelling
Include a clear call-to-action
Match what the searcher's looking for
Highlight what makes you different
Use active voice (not passive)

This Works

"Discover the best running shoes for flat feet. Our expert reviews feature top picks with superior arch support. Shop now and enjoy free shipping on orders over $100."

Compelling, keyword-rich, and has a clear CTA

This Doesn't

"Welcome to our website. We have many products for you to look at. Click on the links to see more information about our running shoes and other items."

Generic, boring, no keywords, no reason to click

Headings: Your Content's Skeleton

Look, proper heading structure isn't just about making things look pretty. It helps both your human readers AND search engines understand how your content is organized. Think of headings as the outline of your page—they tell everyone what matters and how everything fits together. Get this wrong, and you're making it harder for Google to figure out what your content is actually about.

The Heading Hierarchy (Get This Right)

H1

Your Main Page Title

One per page, includes your primary keyword

H2

Main Section Headers

Your major topics and secondary keywords

H3

Subsection Headers

Supporting points that fall under your H2s

H4-H6

Deeper Nested Sections

Use these only if you really need that much organization

Heading Mistakes I See All the Time

  • Skippping heading levels (going straight from H1 to H3)
  • Using multiple H1 tags on a single page
  • Choosing style over actual hierarchy
  • Forgetting to include keywords in your headings
  • Writing headings that don't actually describe the content

Content: Where the Magic Happens

Can we be honest for a second? Quality content is the foundation of everything in SEO. You can optimize your title tags and headings all day long, but if your content isn't actually valuable, you're not going to rank long-term. Google's gotten way too smart for that. Here's how to create content that actually ranks—and keeps people coming back.

What Makes Content "Quality"?

  • Original stuff, not copied from somewhere else
  • Covers the topic thoroughly and completely
  • Accurate info that's actually up-to-date
  • Easy to read and well-structured
  • Brings something unique vs. competitors
  • Scannable with helpful subheadings

How Long Should Content Be?

Blog Posts 1,000-2,500 words
Pillar Pages 3,000-5,000 words
Product Descriptions 300-500 words
Category Pages 300+ words

Making Your Content Actually Readable

Paragraph Length

Keep 'em short: 2-3 sentences max

Sentence Length

Under 20 words keeps people reading

Font Size

At least 16px for body text

Where to Put Your Keywords (In Order of Importance)

Title Tag (Near the Beginning)

This is your highest-priority spot, hands down

2

First 100 Words of Your Content

Early placement tells Google you're relevant

3

Whatever You Do, Don't Stuff Keywords

Image Optimization: The Often-Missed Opportunity

Here's something a lot of people overlook: images can drive a ton of traffic through Google Images and make your overall page quality way better. Plus, well-optimized images keep your page loading fast. Let's make sure you're getting this right.

Alt Text That Actually Works

  • Describe what's actually in the image
  • Include keywords where they fit naturally
  • Keep it under 125 characters
  • For decorative images, use alt=""
  • Be specific: "red running shoes on track"

File Optimization Best Practices

  • Name files descriptively (not IMG_1234.jpg)
  • Compress images (WebP is your friend)
  • Implement lazy loading
  • Use srcset for responsive images
  • Add an image sitemap for Google

What Responsive Images Look Like

<img src="image-800.jpg"
    srcset="image-400.jpg 400w,
          image-800.jpg 800w,
          image-1200.jpg 1200w"
    sizes="(max-width: 600px) 100vw, 50vw"
    alt="Descriptive alt text">

URLs: Keep 'em Clean and Simple

These URLs Work

  • example.com/running-shoes-men
  • example.com/blog/seo-tips-2024
  • example.com/products/blue-coffee-mug

These URLs Don't

  • example.com/page?id=12345
  • example.com/PROD_001_BLUE_MUG_XL
  • example.com/blog/post-title-with-way-too-many-words-in-it-2024

URL Best Practices That Actually Work

  • Stick to lowercase letters
  • Separate words with hyphens, not underscores
  • Keep URLs short (under 60 characters)
  • Include your target keywords
  • Avoid special characters altogether
  • Drop unnecessary stop words
  • Match your page title pretty closely
  • Always use HTTPS protocol

Internal Linking: Connect Your Content

Here's the thing about internal links: they help spread page authority around your site AND help people actually find your content. It's like building a roadmap through your website. Get this right, and both Google and your visitors will thank you.

Contextual Links

Links within your content to related pages

Navigation Links

Menu and footer links to important pages

Breadcrumbs

That handy hierarchical navigation trail

Internal Linking Done Right

  • Use descriptive, helpful anchor text
  • Only link to actually relevant content
  • Vary your anchor text naturally
  • Link from your high-authority pages
  • Fix broken internal links ASAP
  • Don't go crazy with links per page
  • Build content hubs and clusters
  • Use dofollow for internal links

External Linking: It's Okay to Link Out

I know there's this fear that linking externally might send people away or leak your "link juice." But here's the truth: linking to authoritative sources actually boosts your credibility and can help your SEO. Google's pretty clear about this.

Why External Links Actually Help

  • Shows you've done your homework
  • Can build relationships with other sites
  • Gives readers extra value
  • Google uses it for context (E-E-A-T)
  • Might lead to reciprocal links
  • Signals quality content

Smart External Linking Practices

  • Link to sources that are actually authoritative
  • Use anchor text that feels natural
  • Open external links in new tabs (target="_blank")
  • Add rel="nofollow" for paid or untrusted links
  • Keep it reasonable: 2-5 per 1,000 words
  • Check regularly for broken external links

Page Speed & Core Web Vitals

Look, nobody likes waiting for slow websites. Neither does Google. Core Web Vitals are now officially a ranking factor, which means your page speed directly affects your rankings. Here's what you need to aim for.

LCP

Loading

Largest Contentful Paint

Shoot for: Under 2.5 seconds

FID

Interactivity

First Input Delay

Shoot for: Under 100ms

CLS

Stability

Cumulative Layout Shift

Shoot for: Under 0.1

Speed Optimization Tips That Work

  • Compress and optimize your images
  • Minify your CSS and JavaScript
  • Enable browser caching
  • Use a CDN for your assets
  • Defer non-critical JavaScript
  • Implement lazy loading
  • Reduce your server response time
  • Minimize HTTP requests

Mobile Optimization: No Longer Optional

Here's a reality check: more people browse on mobile than desktop now. Google knows this, which is why they use mobile-first indexing. That means Google primarily looks at your mobile version when deciding how to rank you. If your mobile experience sucks, your rankings will too.

Mobile-First Indexing: The New Normal

Google mainly uses your mobile version for indexing and ranking these days. Here's what that means for you:

  • Make sure mobile content matches desktop content
  • Use responsive design (it's what Google recommends)
  • Make touch targets at least 44x44 pixels
  • Text should be readable without zooming

User Experience Signals

Here's something a lot of people forget: SEO isn't just about pleasing Google—it's about pleasing actual humans. Google tracks how people interact with your site, and those engagement signals absolutely affect your rankings. If people bounce immediately or don't stick around, Google takes notice.

Metrics That Actually Matter

  • Dwell time (how long people stay)
  • Bounce rate (who leaves immediately)
  • Pages per session
  • Click-through rate from search

UX Best Practices

  • Clear, intuitive navigation
  • Fast loading speeds
  • Readable fonts and proper sizing
  • Helpful internal linking
